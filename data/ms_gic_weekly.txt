INVESTMENT STRATEGY
If the past ﬁve years have produced a learned bias for equity
investors, it is that supply-side shocks generate different outcomes
from demand-side shocks and that most shocks are supply side in
nature. Such assumptions suggest related inﬂationary bouts are
temporary, with demand-destroying price spikes mitigating risks to
Fed policy. This is reinforced by investors’ conviction that we live in an
era of secular, tech-related disinﬂation. But we consider these
assumptions to be overly simplistic and are increasingly convinced
that the current inﬂation regime is fueled by more-sustainable,
demand-driven sources. The AI-infrastructure-demand boom is
disproportionately fostering economic growth, and equities—
extrapolating current conditions to forecast earnings revisions—
signal this optimism. In ﬁxed income, this is showing up in higher real
rates and term premiums, reﬂecting not only more robust natural
growth but greater policy volatility. The boom is also boosting
demand for equity and debt capacity, prompting competition for US
Treasuries as deﬁcits point to ongoing issuance. The recent rates sell-
off certainly makes adding duration to bond portfolios tempting, but
with structural, demand-driven inﬂation potentially still a risk, we’re
inclined to wait. Consider focusing on active equity strategies to
balance passive index exposure. Now is the time to evaluate where
true secular growth (the Mag-7?) may be on relative sale. We are
reducing positions in overbought semiconductor stocks on strength
and maintaining exposure to the diffusion of the AI build-out rather
than the most scarcity-priced epicenters, while emphasizing
diversiﬁcation across real assets and select cyclicals. Hedge funds,
gold, REITs, infrastructure and secondaries remain key allocations.

forecasts have jumped from 13%–15% to 22%–24%. So, what
is going on?
We sense that the divided stock and bond market narratives
come down to differing beliefs about the source of inﬂation
pressures and thus the durability of rate moves. Speciﬁcally,
we see equity investors ﬁrmly in the camp that recent rate
moves are tied to inﬂation expectations linked to a supply-
side shock. Having been conditioned by so-called supply-side
shocks like COVID, the Russia-Ukraine war and tariffs, equity
investors assume that inﬂation impacts are temporary—and
ultimately self-curing as price hikes reduce aggregate demand.
This has left them too focused on a simplistic narrative tying
global market moves only to oil prices. Adding to the
dismissal of inﬂation concerns is deep-seated faith in the
productivity-enhancing power of technology to underpin a
structural disinﬂation tailwind. While we respect this
argument, we think it is incomplete and risks obscuring the
broader regime change in rates and inﬂation unleashed by
monetary and ﬁscal stimulus from 2009 to 2020. Perhaps
more importantly, it risks mischaracterizing different sources
of pricing power—with that accruing from commodity prices
deemed temporary and that achieved by manufacturers in the
tech supply chain (semiconductor makers) deemed durable.
We are seeing this in the extrapolation of upside earnings
surprises to proﬁt forecasts.
We believe recent Treasury market moves in fact frame a
different narrative: Rather than the inﬂation and rate-hike
threat being driven by the temporal situation in the Middle
East, it is a demand phenomenon driven by the powerful AI
boom, ﬁscal stimulus and genuine resource scarcity. This is
conﬁrmed by broad increases in core inﬂation metrics
indicating that, away from energy, prices are rising strongly
for imported goods, manufacturing inputs, industrial
commodities and services. Consider that out of the total
repricing of long-duration rates since mid-May, only around
5% had recently come from an increase in long-run inﬂation
expectations, with 45% from real rates and half from higher
term premiums. This reﬂects not only more robust natural
growth rates but more policy volatility given potentially
greater cyclicality and capital and resource intensity, and thus
policy uncertainty. A recent Federal Reserve Bank of New
York report made similar observations, noting that AI may
actually contribute to intermediate-term inﬂation pressure, as
it competes for scarce resources.  
A ﬁnal point around the demand-driven idea of “crowding
out” and higher capital costs is that the AI boom is also
generating new ﬁnancing demand. Public equity and debt
issuance is on pace to be the highest in years, diverting
interest from Treasuries at a time when developed market
debt and deﬁcits are ballooning, suggesting ongoing issuance.
Consider that US rates recently rose in lockstep with those in
the UK, Europe and Japan over a 10-day period. This creates a
conundrum for new Federal Reserve chair Kevin Warsh, whose
stated ambitions include steepening the yield curve by easing
fed funds and reducing the Fed’s balance sheet through
runoff and duration shortening. A policy of “running it hot”
risks taking Fed accommodation off the table and keeping
downward pressure on stock-valuation multiples.
Bottom Line. We believe that dismissing rate moves as
merely the temporary result of the energy supply shock is
shortsighted and misses larger dynamics. Long rates are not
repricing based on inﬂation expectations but on real rates
and term premiums. Although energy price-driven inﬂation
will likely be short-lived, demand-side forces appear to be
driving structurally higher rates. While the AI boom is apt to
foster meaningful productivity gains, in the short to
intermediate term, the capital-spending surge should
contribute to sustained pricing power across the tech
infrastructure ecosystem, upwardly pressuring nominal
economic growth and equity and debt capacity demand amid
higher developed market deﬁcits. Given these crosscurrents,
Fed chair Warsh’s yield-curve steepening goal may also be
challenged. Reducing the market’s dependence on forward
guidance may prove fraught as well. Investors who recognize
these realities will appreciate that valuation multiples will
be constrained and that the response to diversiﬁed portfolio
challenges increasingly falls on alternative asset classes.
Watch real US Treasury rates and term premiums in addition
to more volatile inﬂation readings to gauge the direction of
rates. Consider focusing on active equity strategies to
balance passive index exposure. Now is the time to evaluate
where true secular growth (the Mag-7?) may be on relative
sale. We are reducing positions in overbought semiconductor
stocks on strength and maintaining exposure to the diffusion
of the AI build-out rather than the most scarcity-priced
epicenters, while emphasizing diversiﬁcation across real
assets and select cyclicals. Hedge funds, gold, REITs,
infrastructure and secondaries remain key allocations.

Chart of the Week: More Forgiving P/E Ratios Obscure Earnings Quality
A common refrain from equity market bulls is that
earnings revisions are outpacing price action, making
price/earnings (P/E) multiples much more reasonable. We
have a problem with that logic. Not only may tech sector
earnings be ﬂattered by unsustainable gains from
temporary pricing power, but earnings quality is
deteriorating via the use of accelerated capex and R&D
expensing and mismatches with actual useful life
depreciation. One of our key earnings quality gauges is
how closely reported earnings track actual cash ﬂows,
and there’s the rub. While tech sector earnings continue
to grow by high double digits, free cash ﬂow is shrinking,
as capex and debt service take larger bites. On a
price/free-cash-ﬂow basis, the Nasdaq-100 Index is anything but reasonably priced—at a ﬁve-year high of 34 times versus 24
based on reported accounting  proﬁts.
Fixed Income Insight: We Are in the Midst of a Bond Bear Market
It’s easy to understand that on any given day, interest
rates are on the margin driven by incremental news ﬂow
around growth, inﬂation, policy or uncertainty risk. But
when making calls about portfolio construction and bond
duration, it is critical to understand the longer-term
regime. We have high conviction that the rate and
inﬂation regime fundamentally changed from the 2009–
2019 era of ﬁnancial repression with policy responses to
COVID in 2020—catalyzing the end of the 42-year bond
bull market. Examining US Treasury market returns over
recent years versus those of other drawdowns over the
past 32 years frames the rate reset’s durability
historically. While most Treasury drawdowns and yield
spikes within the context of a bond bull market were fewer than 100 trading days, this one has persisted for more than six
years. That bears respecting!

Tactical Asset Allocation Reasoning
Global Equities Weight Relative
to Model Benchmark  
US Overweight
The disinﬂation boom thesis catalyzed by the GenAI capex buildout, along with aggressive
monetary, ﬁscal and regulatory stimulus, is now very much consensus. Forecasts are ambitious
and embed an expectation for cyclical broadening that takes S&P "493" earnings growth from 6%–
8% in 2025 to 14%–16% in 2026. In a market that is already expensive, concentrated and
complacent, this suggests the aperture for upside surprise is narrow, and markets are apt to be
quite brittle in the face of exogenous shock. With midterm-election-related policy already hitting a
fever pitch, potential for these undiscounted events to occur is going up. Thus, while we are
bullish (with an S&P 500 Index target at 7,500 to 7,800), we prefer stock picking to simply
owning the cap-weighted index. Favor ﬁnancials, health care and select industrials and energy.
International Equities
(Developed Markets) Underweight
Recent outperformance has been catalyzed as responses to the “America First” agenda have
driven ﬁscal stimulus and China's record export push has been cooling rest-of-world (ROW)
inﬂation. This is creating ROW opportunities that may simultaneously enjoy monetary, ﬁscal and
currency-related stimulus. The outlook is improving in Japan as corporate restructuring occurs
alongside a reﬂationary surge three decades in the making.
Emerging Markets Overweight
The US-China trade conﬂict, now in a one-year truce, has created opportunities. China exports are
booming and delivering helpful goods deﬂation to the ROW, where central banks are responding
with rate cuts. Surging industrial commodity prices alongside falling energy prices are a perfect
brew for emerging markets, especially as forces continue to bias the US dollar weaker, helping
dollar-denominated debt servicing. The "new Monroe Doctrine" creates additional opportunities
not only for pro-business political stability in Latin America but for direct investment. For Asia,
strategic realignment opportunities continue to surface as the US turns away. India remains a
recommended secular growth long.
Global Fixed Income Weight Relative
to Model Benchmark  
US Investment Grade Underweight
The Fed easing cycle, including some assumptions around the loss of Fed independence in 2026,
has been baked into the US Treasury yield curve, with another two 25-basis-point rate cuts
discounted. Stealth QE in the form of reserve management purchases of T-bills by the Fed and
MBS QE by Fannie Mae and Freddie Mac are easing ﬁnancial conditions materially, putting
pressure on the long end of the curve as inﬂationary pressures build. A ﬁnal note of caution is our
forecast for a surge in IG corporate issuance, which we see on the back of increasing capex and
rising M&A deal activity. As a result, we are continuing to reduce short-duration exposure and
moving toward the “belly of the curve” to capture decent coupons with lower price volatility. We
see the long end continuing to be plagued by structural imbalances that show up as widening
term premiums, with the two-year/30-year portion of the curve remaining in a steepening pattern.
Opportunistic Fixed Income Market-Weight
High Yield is competing effectively with better liquidity, transparency and credit quality than
private credit. In ROW and emerging market debt, yields are decent, central banks have begun to
cut rates and there is room for spread tightening as economic growth improves. Currency impact
is a tailwind for US dollar investors.
Alternative Investments Weight Relative
to Model Benchmark  
Real Assets Overweight
We expect higher stock-bond correlations, which place a premium on the diversiﬁcation beneﬁts
of investing in real assets. Nevertheless, with real interest rates positive and services inﬂation
remaining quite sticky, we would need to be selective in adding to this asset class broadly. We are
focused on industrial metals, energy infrastructure and interesting opportunities aimed at solving
the residential housing shortage. 
Hedged Strategies Overweight
We recently added to equity hedged positions, noting the pickup in idiosyncratic risk, falling
borrowing costs and rising stock level dispersion. The current environment appears constructive
for hedge fund managers, who are frequently good stock pickers and can use leverage and risk
management to potentially amplify returns. We prefer very active and fundamental strategies,
especially high-quality, low-beta, low-volatility and absolute return/market neutral hedge funds.

Glossary
Drawdown refers to the largest cumulative percentage decline in net asset value or the percentage decline from the highest value or net assetvalue (peak) to the lowest value net asset value (trough) after the peak.
Earnings revisions breadth is deﬁned as the number of positive analyst revisions minus the number of negative analyst revisions divided by thetotal number of revisions.
Equity risk premium is the excess return that an individual stock or the overall stock market provides over a risk-free rate. The risk-free raterepresents the interest an investor would expect from an absolutely risk-free investment over a speciﬁed period of time.
Illiquidity premium is the extra yield investors expect to earn for giving up control to liquidate their capital for a certain period of time.
M2 is a measure of the money supply that includes all elements of M1 as well as "near money." M1 includes cash and checking deposits, whilenear money refers to savings deposits, money market securities, mutual funds and other time deposits.
Option-adjusted spread (OAS) is a measurement of the spread of a ﬁxed income security rate and the risk-free rate of return, which is adjustedto take into account an embedded option.
Price to forward earnings calculates the price-to-earnings ratio that uses projected future earnings.
Risk premium is the return in excess of the risk-free rate of return an investment is expected to yield.
Term premium is the excess yield that investors require to commit to holding a long-term bond instead of a series of shorter-term bonds.
Volatility This is a statistical measure of the dispersion of returns for a given security or market index. Volatility can either be measured by usingthe standard deviation or variance between returns from that same security or market index. Commonly, the higher the volatility, the riskier thesecurity.

MLPs
Master Limited Partnerships (MLPs) are limited partnerships or limited liability companies that are taxed as partnerships and whose interests(limited partnership units or limited liability company units) are traded on securities exchanges like shares of common stock. Currently, mostMLPs operate in the energy, natural resources or real estate sectors. Investments in MLP interests are subject to the risks generally applicableto companies in the energy and natural resources sectors, including commodity pricing risk, supply and demand risk, depletion risk andexploration risk. Individual MLPs are publicly traded partnerships that have unique risks related to their structure. These include, but are notlimited to, their reliance on the capital markets to fund growth, adverse ruling on the current tax treatment of distributions (typically mostlytax deferred), and commodity volume risk. The potential tax beneﬁts from investing in MLPs depend on their being treated as partnerships forfederal income tax purposes and, if the MLP is deemed to be a corporation, then its income would be subject to federal taxation at the entitylevel, reducing the amount of cash available for distribution to the fund which could result in a reduction of the fund’s value.
MLPs carry interest rate risk and may underperform in a rising interest rate environment. MLP funds accrue deferred income taxes for future taxliabilities associated with the portion of MLP distributions considered to be a tax-deferred return of capital and for any net operating gains aswell as capital appreciation of its investments; this deferred tax liability is reﬂected in the daily NAV; and, as a result, the MLP fund’s after-taxperformance could differ signiﬁcantly from the underlying assets even if the pre-tax performance is closely tracked.
