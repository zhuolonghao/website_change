INVESTMENT STRATEGY
For most of the past month, the S&P 500 has vacillated in a trading
range, but three relevant uncertainties have now passed, and investor
ﬂows suggest renewed conviction in the bullish narrative that the US
economy is headed back to a tech-led disinﬂationary boom. To wit,
the apparent agreement on reopening the Strait of Hormuz, Federal
Reserve Chair Warsh’s debut and the digestion of historic stock and
bond issuance have emboldened investors. Market action, however,
suggests little nuance, even with much unresolved. Take the certainty
with which investors have priced the strait’s reopening, as December
crude futures are back within 15% of their pre-war level despite
prospects for oil market normalization to take months. Meanwhile,
inﬂation breakeven rates have fallen sharply. On the opposite side,
bond investors appear to have gained certainty after last week’s Fed
meeting implied a hawkish bias, with the probability of a 2026 rate
hike priced above 50%. Finally, there was the record-breaking SpaceX
IPO. While investors can be reassured by availability of longer-term
capital, few are questioning, as we are, if the days of a public-equity
scarcity premium are ending. Resilient earnings will likely carry the
day a bit longer, but we are focused on the implications of higher real
rates, wider risk premiums, lower equity valuations, potentially sticky
inﬂation and more-volatile monetary policy, with key issues far from
resolved. Consider balancing passive US index exposure with active
stock-picking or equal-weighted exposure while increasing selectivity
around capital and ﬁnancing needs, with a preference for maximum
sector and regional diversiﬁcation. We favor EM and Japan and prefer
short-term rates to credit, with portfolio ballast from hedge funds,
real assets, venture capital and growth equity.
One of the hardest lessons to learn as an investor is that markets are not right or
wrong. Rather, they are a reﬂection of the “wisdom of crowds” at a given moment
—an assessment of risks, opportunities and sentiment. Navigating this requires a
willingness to observe what is apparently discounted, while sussing out points of
validation or conﬂict; incoming data that matches what is priced; and potential for

With markets zigzagging around three headline-dominating
anxieties for much of the past month—war in the Middle
East; implications of a new Fed chair; and the potential debut
of extraordinarily large, high-proﬁle “unicorns” in the IPO
market—the S&P 500 Index has been unable to surpass its
all-time high. Thus, it is understandable to think that clarity
on all three might engender market resolution. And at ﬁrst
glance, that has happened, amid record fund inﬂows from
individuals whose “relief” has seen them rush back into the
disinﬂationary AI-boom narrative. Violent market moves
driven by relief suggest “animal spirits” but not necessarily
rational resolution, however. These moves have included
nearly a 30% drop in the spot price for oil, a resetting of one-
year inﬂation swaps, a 15-basis-point jump in the real
component of the two-year US Treasury yield and a radical
shift in 2026 rate-hike projections to more than 1.5, not to
mention an initial surge in IPO valuations. Although
discounted scenarios may prove directionally accurate, we
have a more cautiously optimistic view; indeed, complexity
and nuance may matter this summer as conviction in the
disinﬂationary-boom scenario is retested.
Consider ﬁrst the potential scenarios around reopening the
Strait of Hormuz. In The GIC Weekly of June 8, we shared
Morgan Stanley & Co.’s oil strategists’ argument for why oil
prices might stay elevated for longer, even with immediate
implementation of diplomatic solutions. While it is possible
that demand destroyed by surging prices never returns,
shipping lanes quickly clear and supply chains restabilize, do
we really not expect oil prices to preserve some risk
premium? Will damaged production facilities be restored to
full capacity by year-end? How likely is it that low prices
won’t immediately be met by demand to reﬁll inventories?
This says nothing of increasing demand from rebounding
global growth. Lower oil prices matter to inﬂation, but they
are not the only thing that matters, and current “disinﬂation
euphoria” priced into equities may have overshot.
Next is the reading of Fed Chair Warsh’s debut as hawkish. It
is almost ironic that he made clear he does not believe in
extensive forward guidance and that his tenure will be
marked by change in this regard. He chose not to submit to
the Summary of Economic Projection’s “dot plot” or the PCE
forecast; yet, those are what seemed to drive the jump in the
two-year Treasury yield and curve ﬂattening. Rather than
focus on potential inﬂation risks, equity investors celebrated
the validation that growth is coming in better than hoped for.
By emphasizing his unwavering commitment to the 2%
inﬂation target, Warsh was able to keep the long end of the
yield curve anchored and the US dollar bid—an admirable
feat to battle inﬂation given that tech-linked AI imports are a
key source of pricing pressure. This occurred amid avoidance
of the debate on stagﬂation risks, which are implied by the
same ﬂat two-year/10-year yield curve that has collapsed to
only 28 basis points. In fact, the two-year yield is at a one-
year high, at 4.2%, having decoupled from oil prices (see Fixed
Income Insight). Finally, the market chose to ignore
implications of a Fed committed to be more data-driven and
less forecast-driven for the term premium, even with Chair
Warsh intimating that the central bank’s sources for data
might be changing meaningfully. This transition of the policy-
setting mechanism suggests more volatility and wider
spreads.
Last but not least is the relief investors could claim after the
much-anticipated equity and debt issuance, amid seemingly
unlimited long-term capital availability. While signs of stress
are fairly scarce, hyperscaler relative underperformance could
indicate that positive free cash ﬂow still matters. With
corporate “savings,” or cash levels, back near pre-COVID
levels and the household savings rate plummeting to 2.6%,
we must begin to acknowledge that capital is limited. In fact,
based on our proprietary read of Morgan Stanley client
portfolio positions, cash is at a decade low. That is especially
true in a multipolar world where global debt and deﬁcits are
growing. Not only is the public-equity scarcity premium
declining for the ﬁrst time in many decades, but the pressure
on real global rates is increasingly structural.
Bottom Line. After a volatile and range-bound month, last
week provided some clarity on “known unknowns.” With a
signed memorandum of agreement between the US and Iran,
markets assumed a complete reopening of the Strait of
Hormuz and rapid normalization of oil prices and inﬂation,
which we see as potential oversimpliﬁcation. At the same
time, the bond market’s reaction to Chair Warsh’s FOMC
debut may be excessive in the hawkish direction. That said,
equity investor willingness to shrug off the potential
implications of higher real rates and less-accommodative
monetary policy may prove cavalier. Finally, the market’s
ability to easily digest record stock and bond issuance is
heartening but should not be confused with unlimited
capital. Investors would be prudent to watch for signs of
widening risk premiums, higher real rates and scarcity
premiums as savings rates in the US plummet. Corporate
earnings and cash ﬂow will take on added importance as
valuation multiples are pressured. Be selective. Watch
nominal rate dynamics between real rates, term premiums
and inﬂation breakevens. Consider balancing passive US
index exposure with active stock-picking or equal-weighted
exposure while increasing selectivity around capital and
ﬁnancing needs, with a preference for maximum sector and
regional diversiﬁcation. We favor EM and Japan and prefer
short-term rates to credit, with portfolio ballast from hedge
funds, real assets, venture capital and growth equity.

Chart of the Week: Corporate Revenue and Proﬁt Forecasts Diverging
Equity prices have powered ahead in 2026 on the
strength of earnings revisions. Consensus estimates of
S&P 500 Index proﬁt growth have surged from 13%-15%
in January to 23%–24% currently. Curiously, this has
occurred as top-line revenue-growth estimates have
begun to ease, with growth seen falling from around 10%
in the ﬁrst quarter of 2026 to roughly half that rate by
the end of next year. The implication is even stronger
productivity growth, operating leverage and proﬁt
margins. We caution that this “Goldilocks” forecast of
cooling growth supporting disinﬂation is at odds with the
Federal Reserve’s just-released forecasts and bond market
pricing. While the equity market may be right, it feels like
threading a needle.
Fixed Income Insight: Oil and Yields Decoupling
The strong correlation between oil prices and front-end
rates has been among the more confusing dynamics for
investors in the past four months. The relationship caused
some to conclude that anxieties around oil and inﬂation
were the main drivers pressuring the two-year US
Treasury yield higher. As a memorandum of agreement
between the US and Iran has catalyzed a material
pullback in oil prices, however, rates have not followed
suit, raising the question of what else is supporting them.
Policy expectations are key, and with growth running hot
and pricing pressures building from inside the AI-capex
ecosystem, investors are contending with the potential
that “this isn’t just about oil.” This creates uncertainty—a
factor we may be dealing with for the rest of the year.

Tactical Asset Allocation Reasoning
Global Equities Weight Relative
to Model Benchmark  
US Overweight
The productivity-boom thesis catalyzed by the GenAI-capex build-out, along with
aggressive ﬁscal and regulatory stimulus, has become very much consensus. Forecasts are
ambitious and embed an expectation for cyclical broadening that takes "S&P 493" earnings
growth from 6%–8% in 2025 to 24%–26% in 2026. In a market that is already expensive,
concentrated and complacent, this suggests the aperture for upside surprise is narrow, and
markets are apt to be quite brittle in the face of exogenous shocks. With midterm-election-
related policy already hitting a fever pitch, potential for these undiscounted events to
occur is rising. Thus, while we are bullish (with MS & Co.'s 2Q2027 S&P 500 Index target at
8,300), we prefer stock-picking to simply owning the cap-weighted index. Favor ﬁnancials,
health care, select industrials and energy.
International Equities
(Developed Markets) Underweight
Fiscal stimulus, a response to the "America First" agenda, has helped to drive recent
outperformance. This is creating rest-of-world (ROW) opportunities that may
simultaneously enjoy monetary, ﬁscal and currency-related stimulus. The outlook is
improving in Japan, as corporate restructuring occurs alongside a reﬂationary surge three
decades in the making.
Emerging Markets Overweight
The "new Monroe Doctrine" creates additional opportunities not only for pro-business
political stability in Latin America, but also for direct investment. For Asia, strategic
realignment opportunities continue to surface, as the US turns away. The US-China trade
conﬂict, though now effectively in a one-year truce, has created opportunities. India
remains a recommended secular growth long.
Global Fixed Income Weight Relative
to Model Benchmark  
US Investment Grade Underweight
Solid growth, supported by ﬁscal stimulus, and inﬂationary pressures may support a
higher-for-longer interest rate environment. Another note of caution is our forecast for a
surge in IG corporate issuance, which we see on the back of increasing capex and rising
M&A activity. As a result, we are continuing to reduce short-duration exposure and moving
toward the “belly of the curve” to capture decent coupons with lower price volatility. We
see the long end continuing to be plagued by structural imbalances that show up as
widening term premiums.
Opportunistic Fixed Income Market-Weight
High yield is competing effectively, with better liquidity, greater transparency and more
favorable credit quality than private credit. In ROW and emerging market debt, yields are
decent, and there is room for spread tightening, as economic growth improves.
Alternative Investments Weight Relative
to Model Benchmark  
Real Assets Overweight
We expect higher stock-bond correlations, which place a premium on the diversiﬁcation
beneﬁts of investing in real assets. Nevertheless, with real interest rates positive and
services inﬂation remaining quite sticky, we would need to be selective in adding to this
asset class broadly. We are focused on industrial metals, energy infrastructure and
interesting opportunities aimed at solving the residential housing shortage. 
Hedged Strategies Overweight
We recently added to equity hedged positions, noting the pickup in idiosyncratic risk and
rising stock-level dispersion. The current environment appears constructive for hedge fund
managers, who are frequently good stock-pickers and can use leverage and risk
management to potentially amplify returns. We prefer very active and fundamental
strategies, especially high-quality, low-beta, low-volatility and absolute return/market
neutral strategies.

Glossary
Drawdown refers to the largest cumulative percentage decline in net asset value or the percentage decline from the highest value or net assetvalue (peak) to the lowest value net asset value (trough) after the peak.
Earnings revisions breadth is deﬁned as the number of positive analyst revisions minus the number of negative analyst revisions divided by thetotal number of revisions.
Equity risk premium is the excess return that an individual stock or the overall stock market provides over a risk-free rate. The risk-free raterepresents the interest an investor would expect from an absolutely risk-free investment over a speciﬁed period of time.
Illiquidity premium is the extra yield investors expect to earn for giving up control to liquidate their capital for a certain period of time.
M2 is a measure of the money supply that includes all elements of M1 as well as "near money." M1 includes cash and checking deposits, whilenear money refers to savings deposits, money market securities, mutual funds and other time deposits.
Option-adjusted spread (OAS) is a measurement of the spread of a ﬁxed income security rate and the risk-free rate of return, which is adjustedto take into account an embedded option.
Price to forward earnings calculates the price-to-earnings ratio that uses projected future earnings.
Risk premium is the return in excess of the risk-free rate of return an investment is expected to yield.
Term premium is the excess yield that investors require to commit to holding a long-term bond instead of a series of shorter-term bonds.
Volatility This is a statistical measure of the dispersion of returns for a given security or market index. Volatility can either be measured by usingthe standard deviation or variance between returns from that same security or market index. Commonly, the higher the volatility, the riskier thesecurity.

MLPs
Master Limited Partnerships (MLPs) are limited partnerships or limited liability companies that are taxed as partnerships and whose interests(limited partnership units or limited liability company units) are traded on securities exchanges like shares of common stock. Currently, mostMLPs operate in the energy, natural resources or real estate sectors. Investments in MLP interests are subject to the risks generally applicableto companies in the energy and natural resources sectors, including commodity pricing risk, supply and demand risk, depletion risk andexploration risk. Individual MLPs are publicly traded partnerships that have unique risks related to their structure. These include, but are notlimited to, their reliance on the capital markets to fund growth, adverse ruling on the current tax treatment of distributions (typically mostlytax deferred), and commodity volume risk. The potential tax beneﬁts from investing in MLPs depend on their being treated as partnerships forfederal income tax purposes and, if the MLP is deemed to be a corporation, then its income would be subject to federal taxation at the entitylevel, reducing the amount of cash available for distribution to the fund which could result in a reduction of the fund’s value.
MLPs carry interest rate risk and may underperform in a rising interest rate environment. MLP funds accrue deferred income taxes for future taxliabilities associated with the portion of MLP distributions considered to be a tax-deferred return of capital and for any net operating gains aswell as capital appreciation of its investments; this deferred tax liability is reﬂected in the daily NAV; and, as a result, the MLP fund’s after-taxperformance could differ signiﬁcantly from the underlying assets even if the pre-tax performance is closely tracked.
