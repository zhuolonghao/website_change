INVESTMENT STRATEGY
One of the second quarter’s bigger surprises has been that, despite
disappointment regarding reopening of the Strait of Hormuz, oil has
stayed in a tight range, with West Texas Intermediate (WTI) crude
20% below its April high. Given negative equity and bond correlations
with the commodity during the Iran conﬂict, this has been a virtual
gift to investors. As with tariffs last year, one might assume investors
have “moved on” from concerns about oil. Some unique
circumstances, however, have supported the oil market’s relative
calm. Consider that global inventories were at a ﬁve-year high
entering the conﬂict; the US has temporarily stepped up exports; and
global strategic reserves have been quickly released. Unfortunately,
those factors are not timeless, and complacency on oil, inﬂation and
rates could still be a risk. With inventories normalizing, the Strategic
Petroleum Reserve declining and near-term alternative supply growth
limited, vigilance is warranted. That said, Morgan Stanley & Co.
recently raised its Brent crude outlook for roughly the next six to nine
months to $85–$95 per barrel. Proﬁt margin forecasts and the
consumer could be most vulnerable. Consider maintaining measured
exposure to consumer equities and stocks for which commodities
disinﬂation is embedded in margin forecasts, while adding to energy
and other commodities as a hedge. Focus on active equity strategies
to balance passive index exposure. Now is the time to evaluate where
true secular growth (the Mag-7?) may be on relative sale. Continue
reducing overbought semiconductors on strength, favoring AI build-
out diffusion over scarcity-priced epicenters. Emphasize diversiﬁcation
across real assets, as hedge funds, gold, REITs, infrastructure and
secondaries remain key allocations.

Treasury yields initially reacted, pushing US equities down as
much as 9% in March. And yes, WTI crude is up 57% for the
year to date, US gas prices were recently up more than 45%,
inﬂation has jumped above 3% and the two-year Treasury
yield has risen nearly 70 basis points. But market conﬁdence
that this will be temporary has been unwavering, with WTI
spot prices and December 2026 futures contracts reﬂecting
major backwardation of the futures curve and assumptions of
rapid price normalization. Most recently, despite progress on
the strait proving elusive, WTI has settled back down, off
20% from its April high. As shown in Chart of the Week, such
market conﬁdence has been a gift to equities, removing a
potential key headwind, as stock and bond correlations with
oil have been strongly negative. While stock owners have
been buoyed by robust earnings, positive forecast revisions
and constructive ﬁnancial conditions, in turn supporting
capital markets activity, investors should not presume that
current oil price optimism is permanent.
For starters, we need to appreciate the uniqueness of the
situation that has helped buffer the global economy. When
the crisis began, world oil supply was ample. In January, WTI
crude ranged from $55 to $65 per barrel, with global
inventories their highest in ﬁve years. As the MS & Co.
Research energy team has suggested, not only was the oil
market in surplus, but “oil on the water” was elevated.
Second, China was especially well inventoried. In fact, the
team notes that seaborne net imports into China have fallen
from approximately 13 million barrels per day this time last
year to around 7.5 million per day in the past 30 days.
Meanwhile, net US oil exports have increased from
approximately 5 million barrels per day to 9 million, helped
by a release from the Strategic Petroleum Reserve. Speaking
of strategic reserves, the International Energy Agency
announced a coordinated release of 400 million barrels from
member countries in March. This amounts to about 2.5 million
barrels per day, which when added to roughly 12.5 million in
daily global inventory drawdowns, nearly matches the 14.5
million barrels per day analysts estimate are impaired. These
shock absorbers were further enhanced by ongoing sanctions
relief for Russian and select Iranian shipments, not to mention
global ﬁscal support to mitigate price pressures. We have also
been helped by relatively strong demand destruction,
especially around jet fuel.
But those supports are aging, and some analysts suggest the
clock is ticking on reopening the strait without renewed
upward pressure on global prices. Speciﬁcally, US oil
inventories have been declining sharply. Diesel and gasoline
inventories are already well below ﬁve-year seasonal lows,
and on the current trajectory, they are likely to test minimum
operational requirements later this summer. Second,
according to MS & Co. Research, cumulative crude-oil-
purchase deals closed by Chinese companies for June and July
delivery are still unusually low. This implies ﬁve consecutive
months of low imports to China, and with that, likely
sustained inventory draws. According to the team, there is a
meaningful chance that Chinese crude buyers will return to
the market for the September delivery cycle in mid-to-late
June, stimulating demand. Third, global strategic petroleum
reserve releases are set to fall from approximately 2.5 million
barrels per day in the April to June period to approximately
0.7 million per day in July and August. If the strait is not
reopened by the time of this “SPR cliff,” the market could
tighten meaningfully.
While markets have clearly reacted to moves in oil spot prices
and near-month futures contracts, we believe the scenarios
being priced might still be overly optimistic. In fact, per the
assessment of MS & Co. Research strategists, delays in
reopening the strait push out likely market normalization by
as much as three to four months. This has caused them to
adjust their outlook for December futures, raising Brent crude
pricing for roughly the next six to nine months to $85–$95
per barrel, around $10 ahead of market pricing, as
normalization continues to get pushed well into late summer.
If this occurs, we anticipate that stock and bond markets
could both face headwinds, the Federal Reserve would have
to grapple with more inﬂation concerns, and consumers and
corporate margins could experience stress. The asymmetric
risk of this stagﬂation scenario is not fully priced.
Bottom Line. Despite few signs of actual progress, oil spot
prices and six-month futures contracts have reﬂected
optimism that a Strait of Hormuz reopening is around the
corner. Benign price action has been a major tailwind for risk
assets given negative stock and bond correlations with oil.
With equity market euphoria escalating, we note that factors
cushioning the oil shock—global inventories, strategic
reserve releases and ﬁscal support—may be evaporating.
MS & Co. strategists have pushed out forecasts for market
normalization and raised their oil price outlook for roughly
the next six to nine months, with potential implications for
inﬂation and rates. The capex boom and proﬁt/productivity
cycle may be compelling, but it is not bulletproof, and with
regard to oil, we’re not out of the woods yet; investors
should avoid complacency. Watch oil futures pricing and
pass-through to inﬂation expectations and rates. Consider
maintaining measured exposure to consumer equities and
stocks for which commodities disinﬂation is embedded in
margin forecasts, while adding to energy and other
commodities as a hedge. Focus on active equity strategies to
balance passive index exposure. Now is the time to evaluate
where true secular growth (the Mag-7?) may be on relative
sale. Continue reducing overbought semiconductors on
strength, favoring AI build-out diffusion over scarcity-priced
epicenters. Emphasize diversiﬁcation across real assets, as
hedge funds, gold, REITs, infrastructure and secondaries
remain key allocations.

Chart of the Week: Strait of Hormuz Still Matters
Market narratives appear to have moved on from daily
concerns about a deal to reopen the Strait of Hormuz, as
the economy has proved resilient to the oil price jump
and investors have grown wary of headlines. That said, a
wider perspective suggests easing oil prices may be
critical to sustaining a rally in stocks and bonds, as both
have been strongly negatively correlated with oil. Not
only do higher oil prices translate into inﬂation and
impact monetary policy, but the petrochemical supply
chain has implications for proﬁt margins and consumer
demand. A deal to reopen the strait may be delayed and
forgotten about for now, but with buffers—global oil
inventories, strategic petroleum reserves, US exports—
that have cushioned the blow decaying, stress around the lack of a deal may resurface.
Fixed Income Insight: Watch the 10-Year Yield for Equity “Broadening”
With equity indexes hitting new highs over the past 10
weeks, poor market breadth may be surprising, as
outperformers have been concentrated in a handful of
sectors linked to the AI-capex boom. The share of stocks
above their 200-day moving average is only around 50%–
55%, fairly unimpressive amid a bull market, when it
usually hits 70%–80%. One headwind to improved
breadth continues to be the 10-year US Treasury yield—
key to valuations and cost-of-capital considerations. With
investors contemplating a higher neutral rate and term
premiums amid swollen budget deﬁcits, along with hand-
wringing over inﬂation, the 10-year yield has hovered
around 4.5%, a level that has been an impediment for
most of this cycle. We continue to expect breadth to improve, but lower rates may be a requisite catalyst that is not imminent.

Tactical Asset Allocation Reasoning
Global Equities Weight Relative
to Model Benchmark  
US Overweight
The productivity-boom thesis catalyzed by the GenAI-capex build-out, along with
aggressive ﬁscal and regulatory stimulus, has become very much consensus. Forecasts are
ambitious and embed an expectation for cyclical broadening that takes "S&P 493" earnings
growth from 6%–8% in 2025 to 24%–26% in 2026. In a market that is already expensive,
concentrated and complacent, this suggests the aperture for upside surprise is narrow, and
markets are apt to be quite brittle in the face of exogenous shocks. With midterm-election-
related policy already hitting a fever pitch, potential for these undiscounted events to
occur is rising. Thus, while we are bullish (with MS & Co.'s 2Q2027 S&P 500 Index target at
8,300), we prefer stock-picking to simply owning the cap-weighted index. Favor ﬁnancials,
health care, select industrials and energy.
International Equities
(Developed Markets) Underweight
Fiscal stimulus, a response to the "America First" agenda, has helped to drive recent
outperformance. This is creating rest-of-world (ROW) opportunities that may
simultaneously enjoy monetary, ﬁscal and currency-related stimulus. The outlook is
improving in Japan, as corporate restructuring occurs alongside a reﬂationary surge three
decades in the making.
Emerging Markets Overweight
The "new Monroe Doctrine" creates additional opportunities not only for pro-business
political stability in Latin America, but also for direct investment. For Asia, strategic
realignment opportunities continue to surface, as the US turns away. The US-China trade
conﬂict, though now effectively in a one-year truce, has created opportunities. India
remains a recommended secular growth long.
Global Fixed Income Weight Relative
to Model Benchmark  
US Investment Grade Underweight
Solid growth, supported by ﬁscal stimulus, and inﬂationary pressures may support a
higher-for-longer interest rate environment. Another note of caution is our forecast for a
surge in IG corporate issuance, which we see on the back of increasing capex and rising
M&A activity. As a result, we are continuing to reduce short-duration exposure and moving
toward the “belly of the curve” to capture decent coupons with lower price volatility. We
see the long end continuing to be plagued by structural imbalances that show up as
widening term premiums.
Opportunistic Fixed Income Market-Weight
High yield is competing effectively, with better liquidity, greater transparency and more
favorable credit quality than private credit. In ROW and emerging market debt, yields are
decent, and there is room for spread tightening, as economic growth improves.
Alternative Investments Weight Relative
to Model Benchmark  
Real Assets Overweight
We expect higher stock-bond correlations, which place a premium on the diversiﬁcation
beneﬁts of investing in real assets. Nevertheless, with real interest rates positive and
services inﬂation remaining quite sticky, we would need to be selective in adding to this
asset class broadly. We are focused on industrial metals, energy infrastructure and
interesting opportunities aimed at solving the residential housing shortage. 
Hedged Strategies Overweight
We recently added to equity hedged positions, noting the pickup in idiosyncratic risk and
rising stock-level dispersion. The current environment appears constructive for hedge fund
managers, who are frequently good stock-pickers and can use leverage and risk
management to potentially amplify returns. We prefer very active and fundamental
strategies, especially high-quality, low-beta, low-volatility and absolute return/market
neutral strategies.

Glossary
Drawdown refers to the largest cumulative percentage decline in net asset value or the percentage decline from the highest value or net assetvalue (peak) to the lowest value net asset value (trough) after the peak.
Earnings revisions breadth is deﬁned as the number of positive analyst revisions minus the number of negative analyst revisions divided by thetotal number of revisions.
Equity risk premium is the excess return that an individual stock or the overall stock market provides over a risk-free rate. The risk-free raterepresents the interest an investor would expect from an absolutely risk-free investment over a speciﬁed period of time.
Illiquidity premium is the extra yield investors expect to earn for giving up control to liquidate their capital for a certain period of time.
M2 is a measure of the money supply that includes all elements of M1 as well as "near money." M1 includes cash and checking deposits, whilenear money refers to savings deposits, money market securities, mutual funds and other time deposits.
Option-adjusted spread (OAS) is a measurement of the spread of a ﬁxed income security rate and the risk-free rate of return, which is adjustedto take into account an embedded option.
Price to forward earnings calculates the price-to-earnings ratio that uses projected future earnings.
Risk premium is the return in excess of the risk-free rate of return an investment is expected to yield.
Term premium is the excess yield that investors require to commit to holding a long-term bond instead of a series of shorter-term bonds.
Volatility This is a statistical measure of the dispersion of returns for a given security or market index. Volatility can either be measured by usingthe standard deviation or variance between returns from that same security or market index. Commonly, the higher the volatility, the riskier thesecurity.

MLPs
Master Limited Partnerships (MLPs) are limited partnerships or limited liability companies that are taxed as partnerships and whose interests(limited partnership units or limited liability company units) are traded on securities exchanges like shares of common stock. Currently, mostMLPs operate in the energy, natural resources or real estate sectors. Investments in MLP interests are subject to the risks generally applicableto companies in the energy and natural resources sectors, including commodity pricing risk, supply and demand risk, depletion risk andexploration risk. Individual MLPs are publicly traded partnerships that have unique risks related to their structure. These include, but are notlimited to, their reliance on the capital markets to fund growth, adverse ruling on the current tax treatment of distributions (typically mostlytax deferred), and commodity volume risk. The potential tax beneﬁts from investing in MLPs depend on their being treated as partnerships forfederal income tax purposes and, if the MLP is deemed to be a corporation, then its income would be subject to federal taxation at the entitylevel, reducing the amount of cash available for distribution to the fund which could result in a reduction of the fund’s value.
MLPs carry interest rate risk and may underperform in a rising interest rate environment. MLP funds accrue deferred income taxes for future taxliabilities associated with the portion of MLP distributions considered to be a tax-deferred return of capital and for any net operating gains aswell as capital appreciation of its investments; this deferred tax liability is reﬂected in the daily NAV; and, as a result, the MLP fund’s after-taxperformance could differ signiﬁcantly from the underlying assets even if the pre-tax performance is closely tracked.
