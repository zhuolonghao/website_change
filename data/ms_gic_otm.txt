INVESTMENT STRATEGY
Nearly four years into the GenAI bull market and 16 months into the
current presidential administration, investors have developed strong
instincts not only to buy the dips but to do so when they coincide
with as little as a change in tone in White House headlines. This was
the case again in April when the announcement of a ceaseﬁre in Iran
unleashed a massive US equity repositioning rally. 
Over the month, the S&P 500 Index gained more than 10%, the Nasdaq Composite
Index more than 15% and semiconductor stocks nearly 40%. At one point, US
equities rose more than 12% in 14 days—registering in the 99th percentile of
occurrences over the past 100 years. These moves didn’t take place after a
recession or bear market; rather, they followed ﬁve months when the benchmark
index barely registered a correction. Granted, markets had been climbing a wall of
worry—with hand-wringing over AI spending, software-based business models,
private credit, oil prices and inﬂation. But what made April’s move so surprising was
its ferocity—favoring momentum-oriented factors, technical repositioning and the
pivot from macro concerns to micro, with intense focus on an especially strong
earnings season. This entailed rotating back to the winners of October 2022–
October 2025. Over the month, earnings realization and forecast revisions took
expected 2026 S&P 500 earnings growth from 13%–15% to closer to 17%–22%. The
bull case is again fully in charge, with stocks at all-time highs, valuations rich and
expectations ebullient. While stimulus and accelerating AI investment may carry the
day, investors have chosen to “look through” many macro factors as “transitory,”
despite their being worse than they were in the ﬁrst quarter. Oil prices have not yet
come down, personal consumption expenditures (PCE) inﬂation is up 3.2% year over
year and Federal Reserve rate-cut projections for 2026 have fallen to zero amid
signiﬁcantly higher US Treasury yields.
While all these factors could prove transitory, leaving the secular GenAI narrative to
determine market direction, when markets move this far, this fast, we’re inclined to
recalibrate. With the S&P 500 on track to reach Morgan Stanley & Co.’s 2026
target of 7,800, the GIC recommends rebalancing to especially active and diversiﬁed
strategic allocations. In addition to our preference to add to quality names in health
care, ﬁnancials, energy/materials/mining, utilities and REITs, we remain overweight
emerging markets, hedge funds and real assets. Innovation is best accessed through
2026 vintages of venture capital and growth private equity. ■

Last weekend in London was glorious, made even more so by
the eclipsing of a milestone: Yomif Kejelcha of Ethiopia ran
the London Marathon in under two hours, with a time of
1:59:41.
Yet Yomif may not be the name you remember, because
someone else was 11 seconds faster. Sabastian Sawe of Kenya
ran the same 26.2 miles in 1:59:30.
The record-setting pace and the narrow margin between
winner and runner-up feel relevant to the biggest
nongeopolitical story in markets: soaring capital spending on
artiﬁcial intelligence (AI) infrastructure. At heart, this is about
striving to win a technological race run at a record pace, with
enormous stakes.
SPENDING AT HYPER-SCALE. The ﬁrst thing about this
spending is its scale. Morgan Stanley & Co. Research
estimates that US hyperscalers will spend more than $800
billion on capex in 2026. To put that in perspective, it roughly
matches what all the nontechnology companies in the S&P
500 Index spent in 2025 combined.
This spending is also growing rapidly, both outright and
relative to expectations. That roughly $800 billion in
hyperscaler capex we forecast for 2026? It’s almost double
the ﬁgure spent in 2025 and triple the ﬁgure from 2024. For
next year, my colleagues estimate that US hyperscaler capital
spending could hit $1.1 trillion (see chart).

True, part of the increase comes from higher prices for AI
components. But a bigger part of it is simply “more”: more
demand, more chips and more power.
Chips, memory and the clustering of computers have each
become four to seven times more capable in just the past
four years. For 2025–2028, we estimate that the total
compute sold by NVIDIA will grow 210% per year—or a
cumulative 300 times if extrapolated over the next ﬁve years.
But if you build it, will they come?
Google recently noted that it processed 16 billion tokens per
minute for customers, a 60% quarter-over-quarter increase.
An even larger waypoint may arrive soon as a new generation
of AI models trained on much more powerful compute shows
what last year’s investments can (or cannot) do.
IMPLICATIONS FOR MARKETS. All of this spending has big
implications. First, one company’s outlay is another’s revenue,
and many of the stock market’s “winners” have been directly
tied to this historic build-out. In April alone, US
semiconductor stocks rose 38%. The market, and Morgan
Stanley & Co. Research, remains focused on AI beneﬁciaries.
Second, while US tech companies have enormous resources,
these outlays are so large that they still require quite a lot of
borrowing. Our credit strategy teams expect record bond
issuance this year. US tech borrowing is a big part of that. So
far, that’s playing out, with US investment grade supply up
20% year over year. And much of this tech borrowing is
coming with longer-than-average maturities, meaning even
more supply in duration-adjusted terms.
Taken together, we think this presents a worse skew for
credit than equities. Continued strength in spending may
support broader markets but mean heavy credit supply and
upward pressure on spreads. If spending weakens, the hit to
sentiment and risk appetite could catch credit in its wake.
THE THRILL OF COMPETITION. Yet an even stronger thread
running through this story is its competitive nature. The
biggest AI spenders are some of the largest, wealthiest
companies in history. They see AI as a long-term opportunity
with an enormous total addressable market. The stakes, and
the difference between being ﬁrst and fourth, are enormous.
If that seems like a fair assessment, something else follows:
relative price insensitivity. That includes less sensitivity to the
component cost, less sensitivity to the borrowing costs and
less sensitivity to short-term swings in the share price. What
matters is winning a race that still has many miles to run.
How that evolves has macroeconomic implications too. Kevin
Warsh, nominee to be the next Federal Reserve chair, believes
that these investments can boost productivity and justify
lower interest rates.

Resource Nationalism: WhenSecurity Replaces Efﬁciency
Ellen Zentner, Senior Investment Strategist, Morgan Stanley Wealth
Management
Sarah Wolfe, Investment Strategist, Morgan Stanley Wealth Management
Jane Yu Sullivan, CFA, CAIA, Investment Strategist, Morgan Stanley Wealth
Management
Kyle Kerr, Associate, Morgan Stanley Wealth Management
The global economy is shifting away from a decades-long
focus on efﬁciency—including globalized supply chains and
lowest-cost production—toward a system increasingly
deﬁned by security, continuity and resilience. What was once
a simpler supply chain story is now more clearly a story about
geopolitics, infrastructure and power.
A series of shocks has hastened this transition. A breakdown
in logistics during the COVID-19 pandemic exposed how
quickly “just-in-time” inventories can become “just-not-there,”
and this led to “just-in-case” inventory-building patterns.
Russia’s invasion of Ukraine turned energy dependence into a
binding policy constraint. And rising conﬂict risk in the Middle
East, including periodic threats to shipments through the
Strait of Hormuz, has at times put 15%–20% of global oil and
liqueﬁed natural gas supply at risk—a risk that was realized
when war broke out in Iran in late February. Meanwhile, US-
China strategic competition has intensiﬁed, with trade,
technology and industrial policy increasingly treated as
national security tools.
Together, these forces are reshaping how governments and
companies think about risk.
For many strategic inputs, the scarcest asset is not the
resource in the ground, but rather the infrastructure and
permissions required to process, reﬁne and deliver it. Power
grids, processing facilities, logistics networks and permitting
capacity are emerging as the true bottlenecks. For investors,
the opportunity lies in identifying these constraints—where
capacity is hardest to build, slowest to scale and most
valuable in disruption—rather than simply owning or having
access to raw material reserves.
THE JOURNEY TO JUST-IN-CASE. Historically, the term
“resource nationalism” has referred to producer countries
asserting sovereignty over oil or minerals. That dynamic still
exists. But increasingly, the term is about capturing more of
the value chain—processing, reﬁning and manufacturing—
rather than simply exporting raw materials.
Put another way, resource nationalism today is less about
owning resources and more about controlling vulnerability.
Policy has moved accordingly. Export restrictions on industrial
raw minerals have increased more than ﬁvefold between
2009 and 2023, driven largely by China, Vietnam, Burundi
and Russia (see charts). Between 2021 and 2023, more than
20% of global exports across key materials—including cobalt,
rare earth elements, nickel, copper and steel—were subject
to at least one export restriction. In some cases,
concentration has been even more acute: Roughly 67% of
cobalt and 46% of rare earth element exports are controlled.
These measures increasingly span the full value chain—from
extraction to reﬁning—underscoring how strategic these
inputs have become.

roughly six times the mineral inputs of a traditional internal
combustion engine. The International Energy Administration
(IEA) estimates that, under current policy trajectories, critical

Over the past two decades, rising production costs,
environmental constraints, regulatory delays and sustained
Chinese investment in critical minerals pushed the system
toward an “efﬁcient” equilibrium that, in hindsight, looks
fragile. The US moved from relative self-sufﬁciency to import
dependence, while global processing capacity became
increasingly concentrated in China. What once looked
inefﬁcient—redundancy, spare capacity, multiple suppliers—
now looks like necessary insurance.
Instead of “Who can do it cheapest?” the central question has
become, “Who delivers when the system is under stress?”
THE REAL BOTTLENECK. One of the most misunderstood
features of today’s resource landscape is where scarcity sits.
Mining is often geographically diversiﬁed. Processing is not.
China holds the largest reﬁning share for 19 of the 20 major
energy-related raw materials, controlling roughly 40%–100%
of global reﬁning capacity across many critical inputs,
including 90% of rare earths and 60% of lithium capacity (see
chart below). This makes the conversion layer—the step from
raw material to usable industrial input—the true chokepoint
in the global supply chain system.
Processing capacity is slow and difﬁcult to set up. It requires
not just extraordinary amounts of capital, but also energy
infrastructure, skilled labor, environmental permitting and
industrial ecosystems that take decades to build. Just the
upstream process to develop nickel, lithium and copper mines
averages around 17 years, from discovery to construction to
extraction.
The result is a system that can appear diversiﬁed on paper
but remains highly concentrated in practice: Multiple
countries may produce a resource, but only a small number of
processing hubs have the ability to make it usable and at
scale.
AI ACCELERATION. The infrastructure required to support AI
—data centers, semiconductors and power systems—links
digital demand directly back to physical supply chains. As a
result, control over energy, metals and industrial inputs
becomes more valuable, not less, in an AI-driven world.
The competition for energy resources is already visible
globally. But AI also creates a new class of strategic trade
ﬂows layered on top of the old energy-and-minerals map.
According to Federal Reserve research, AI-related goods trade
has grown faster than overall merchandise trade since 2018,
reaching more than $272 billion globally in the ﬁrst half of
2025—a 65% year-over-year increase. The US is the leading
importer for AI-related goods and services.

Demand concentration creates a set of narrow bottlenecks in
advanced servers, GPUs, cooling systems, power
infrastructure and the countries that produce and assemble
them.
For investors, this sharpens the country-level map. Taiwan
remains the highest-value node, with AI-related exports to the
US reaching roughly 14% of its GDP in 2025. After Taiwan,
Mexico and Vietnam are emerging as key assembly and
manufacturing hubs beneﬁting from US-led diversiﬁcation.
US LEADS THE SHIFT. US policy has increasingly focused on
strategic sectors tied to national security and industrial
capacity, including energy, defense, semiconductors and
advanced manufacturing. The objective is clear: Reduce
reliance on foreign supply chains while strengthening the
domestic industrial base.
This builds on a longer arc for the US. The shale revolution
transformed the country from a major energy importer into
the world’s largest oil and gas producer; it reduced net
energy imports from roughly 34% of supply in the mid-2000s
to 16% by 2024. That transformation does not eliminate
exposure to global markets—but it has greatly reduced
vulnerability.
However, the US cannot produce and process everything it
needs. For resources like critical minerals, the country is
looking to increase dependence on closer allies like Canada,
Australia and Argentina. Canada has deposits of more than
half of the minerals on the US “critical list” and is the only
Western nation with all the raw materials needed for
advanced EV batteries. Canada is also attempting to move up
the value chain by expanding both mining and processing.
WHAT HAPPENS IF THE SYSTEM WORKS? An important
question for investors is what the long-term equilibrium
might look like if these strategies succeed.
If supply chains become more diversiﬁed, processing capacity
expands across multiple jurisdictions and redundancy
becomes embedded within the global system, then the
frequency of severe supply disruptions may decline. The
global economy could experience fewer episodes in which a
single chokepoint triggers widespread shortages.
At the same time, the pricing power enjoyed by certain
bottleneck assets during the early stages of the transition
may eventually normalize as additional capacity comes online.
Firms that beneﬁt from scarcity during the build-out phase
may face greater competition once the system becomes more
balanced.
Even in a more stable system, however, the investment cycle
does not end. Infrastructure—including power, grids, logistics
and processing—requires ongoing expansion and
maintenance. That suggests capital intensity may remain
structurally higher than in the late globalization era and, in
turn, drive higher rates of productivity.
INVESTMENT OPPORTUNITY. The most compelling
opportunities in this transition sit at the intersection of
constraint and policy support:
Grid and electriﬁcation enablers
Energy security and reliability
Critical minerals beyond mining
Industrial resilience and automation
Defense production and secure communications
Ultimately, the structural shift toward resource nationalism is
about control. For markets, this does not signal the end of
globalization, but a repricing of how it operates in an
increasingly multipolar world. Supply chains become more
regional, more complex and more costly—at least for a time.
In that environment, the winners are not simply those that
own the resources; the winners will have the infrastructure,
processing capacity and policy alignment to convert resources
into durable economic—and strategic—advantage. ■

US ECONOMICS
Lessons From the Five InnovationWaves Before AI 
Michael T. Gapen, Chief US Economist, Morgan Stanley & Co. LLC
The current artiﬁcial intelligence (AI) wave has led to both
optimism and pessimism: It promises faster productivity and
economic growth but also raises concerns about job
displacement, inequality and social upheaval. In assessing
what AI may mean for the US economy, we look to the past
for guidance. After all, as the saying goes, history may not
repeat itself, but it often rhymes.
Over the past 250 years, the US has experienced ﬁve major
innovation waves:
The First Industrial Revolution 
Steam, railroads and steel
Electriﬁcation and the internal combustion engine
Electronics and aviation
The internet and digital networks
Across these waves, a consistent pattern emerges between
innovation and macroeconomic transformation:
Transformative technologies initially disrupt labor markets
and ﬁnancial systems, provoke political responses and widen
inequality—but they ultimately raise productivity, expand
output and fundamentally reshape the structure of the
economy.
A look at prior innovation waves (see charts) suggests that
while technological revolutions invariably bring short-term
disruptions and uncertainty, they also deliver signiﬁcant long-
run beneﬁts. 
However how those beneﬁts are distributed—narrowly or
broadly—depends on how policymakers, businesses and
educators manage the transition.
Here, we outline the common themes we see across
innovation waves.
FUNDAMENTAL REORGANIZATION OF THE ECONOMY. The
ﬁrst two waves of innovation pulled the country out of its
agrarian roots and into an industrially integrated economy,
connecting regional markets through canals and railroads.
Electriﬁcation and motorization then enabled mass
production and national consumer markets. Electronics and
aviation shifted the economy toward services and science-
based industry. The digital revolution completed the
transition from an agrarian to industrial to knowledge-based
economy built on networks and intangible capital. Each wave
did more than introduce new tools: It rewired how the
economy functioned, where people lived, how ﬁrms organized
production and how value was created. 
EXTRAORDINARY SURGES IN CAPITAL SPENDING.
Innovation waves are never incremental; they have been
correlated with investment booms. Canal construction
absorbed roughly 1% of GDP at its peak, while railroads
commanded up to 2%–3% of GDP annually and more than
10% of gross capital formation. Electriﬁcation required
nationwide utility build-outs, federal mandates and industrial
reconﬁguration. Innovation often requires greater public-
private partnership: The Cold War era saw federal research
and development quadruple as a share of GDP. The digital age
redirected investment from factories and structures toward
software, computing equipment and intellectual property.

Each wave demanded heavy, front-loaded capital deepening,
with early waves focusing on physical capital and structures
and later waves on computers, peripherals and software. The
technologies were capital intensive, network based and
ﬁnanced by optimistic projections of permanent growth.
REALLOCATION IN THE LABOR MARKETS. Each innovation
wave displaced existing occupations and reshufﬂed skill
demand. The First Industrial Revolution reduced the role of
artisans and expanded semi-skilled factory labor. The railroad
and steel era accelerated urbanization and created the
modern managerial class. Electriﬁcation further reduced farm
employment and expanded clerical and professional work.
Postwar electronics and aerospace strengthened technical
occupations while manufacturing employment peaked and
then began to decline. The digital revolution hollowed out
middle-skill routine jobs, pushing employment toward high-
skill professional roles and lower-wage services.
Yet across all ﬁve waves, the total demand for labor did not
collapse. Workers were reallocated rather than rendered
obsolete. Even during severe downturns, there is no evidence
that technological progress permanently raised the natural
rate of unemployment. The composition of work changed;
work itself endured.
PRODUCTIVITY GROWTH. Early diffusion is often slow and
uneven, but once complementary investments and
organizational change take hold, output per worker
accelerates. The First Industrial Revolution generated modest
but rising gains. The steam and steel era doubled productivity
growth rates relative to the prior century. Electriﬁcation and
motorization produced a great leap in the early 20th century,
doubling output per hour in a generation. The postwar period
delivered one of the strongest sustained productivity
expansions in US history. The internet wave produced a sharp
acceleration in the late 1990s, even if gains later moderated.
Across waves, productivity surges are real, but they require
time, diffusion and structural adaptation.
VOLATILITY AND BOOM-BUST CYCLES. The canal boom
collapsed in 1837. Railroad overbuilding fueled the panics of
1873 and 1893. The electriﬁcation era encompassed the Great
Depression. The late 1990s tech boom ended in a dramatic
equity collapse. Each wave exhibited a similar pattern:
exuberant capital formation, speculative ﬁnancing, rising
leverage and eventual retrenchment. Financial markets both
accelerate technological diffusion and amplify the cycle when
expectations adjust. Innovation waves have repeatedly
contained at least one major boom-bust episode. As Milton
Friedman might say, “there is no free lunch.”
POLITICS RESHAPED. Industrialization produced early labor
organization and Progressive Era reforms. The Gilded Age’s
concentration of wealth triggered antitrust and income tax
legislation. The third wave produced the New Deal and labor
protections. The fourth wave’s prosperity supported a
midcentury social compact before later deregulation. The
digital era has reopened debates about antitrust laws, data
governance and market concentration. Technological change
consistently redistributes income and power, and political
institutions eventually respond—often slowly, sometimes
dramatically.
INEQUALITY: A RECOGNIZABLE ARC. Early phases of
innovation often widen disparities as capital deepening and
new skill premiums beneﬁt early adopters. The Gilded Age
exempliﬁed extreme concentration. The mid-20th century
stands out as an exception: Electriﬁcation and mass
production coincided with the “Great Compression,”
supported by unions, progressive taxation and expanding
education. The Digital Revolution reversed that compression.
Since 1980, income and wealth concentration have risen
sharply, driven by returns on capital, skill-based technical
change and public policy choices that reversed Great
Compression-era policy. Innovation itself does not
predetermine inequality: Institutions and public policy
mediate how gains are distributed.
EDUCATION AS AN ADAPTATION MECHANISM. Each
previous wave raised the return on human capital, and each
time, the US expanded education in response. The Common
School Movement followed early industrialization. Land-grant
universities professionalized engineering and agricultural

science. The High School Movement and GI Bill democratized
secondary and higher education. The Digital Age intensiﬁed
demand for STEM (science, technology, engineering and
math) skills and lifelong learning. When education expanded

When skill formation lagged, inequality widened.
ACCELERATING PACE OF DIFFUSION. The Industrial
Revolution took half a century to spread. Railroads and steel
diffused over several decades. Electriﬁcation and
motorization required a generation of complementary
investment. Electronics matured over roughly 40 years. The
internet reached mass adoption in less than 30. Each
successive wave has traveled faster than its predecessor,
reﬂecting better communication, deeper capital markets and
accumulated institutional experience. If this pattern holds, AI
may diffuse faster still.
THE LESSONS. Taken together, the historical record is clear.
Innovation waves are disruptive, capital intensive and volatile.
They displace workers, concentrate gains early and provoke
political backlash. But over time they raise productivity,
restructure labor markets, expand output and—when
institutions adapt—improve living standards broadly. The
speed of diffusion is increasing, compressing adjustment
timelines. The lessons are not about whether transformation
will occur, but about how society manages it.  ■

Gold: Safe Haven or Risk Asset?
Amy Gower, CFA, Commodities Strategist, Morgan Stanley & Co.
International plc+
Gold has underperformed since the Middle East conﬂict
began on Feb. 28. In the month that followed, it fell 14.5%,
compared with a 9% decline for the FTSE All-World Index, a
7.8% decline for the S&P 500 Index and a 3.6% decline for the
US Treasury Total Return Index. By the end of April, equities
had fully rebounded to pre-conﬂict levels, and then some. But
the US Treasury index and gold, still down 2.0% and 14.3%,
respectively, had only recovered a fraction of their losses (see
chart).
The sharp contrast with many prior risk events and
downturns—when the precious metal was a notable
outperformer—has led some to question its “safe-haven”
status. That potential conclusion, however, may oversimplify
why it has earned that status and the roles investors ask it to
perform. Widely studied as a long-term hedge against
inﬂation and currency debasement, and as a safe haven in
periods of crisis, gold has exhibited strong sensitivity to real
rates and monetary policy. These often overlapping factors
have established its standing, but their relative importance to
price direction can change, and this pullback highlights what
happens when one driver dominates.
Gold Has Declined Since the Start of the Iran War
Source:  Bloomberg, Morgan Stanley Wealth Management Global Investment
Ofﬁce as of May 5, 2026
Gold can be a hedge for various scenarios. Gold’s classic
portfolio construction use is as a portfolio hedge. Investors
tend to buy it on the expectation that it will hold its value, or
rise, during a market shock when equities fall and risk
appetite disappears. This is where it has disappointed since
the start of the current conﬂict and why we think investors
may question its safe-haven status. A we have noted, it is
expected to show initial weakness post-shock, as its liquidity
is leveraged to cover margin calls and reallocate capital. But
this alone doesn’t explain the degree to which it has pulled
back.
Another use case for gold has been as an inﬂation hedge. As
demonstrated by this episode, it does not always hedge
against the ﬁrst-round effects of an inﬂation shock. While it is
highly regarded as a hedge against inﬂation, over shorter
horizons, it does not necessarily outperform just because
inﬂation risk rises. When a conﬂict pushes up oil prices and
supply chain risks, gold may look like a clear beneﬁciary, but
this can hurt gold if it translates into higher policy rates than
expected.
Gold has also been viewed as a monetary hedge. Its sensitivity
to monetary policy has taken over as the key price driver
since the start of the Middle East conﬂict, overshadowing its
safe-haven status and removing the beneﬁts of owning it to
hedge against the crisis and inﬂation risks. This has shown
how gold’s price does not just reﬂect the event itself, but
more importantly the policy response that follows. The
conﬂict has also removed some investor appetite to employ
gold as a hedge against ﬁat currency debasement, with the
US dollar reafﬁrming its own safe-haven status, as the US is
generally viewed as more shielded from energy shocks.
This highlights why gold is not necessarily a universal safe
haven and, in our view, is actually more conditional.
Therefore, when one driver fails, it can dominate and control
the price. If investors holding gold were relying on it purely to
hedge against rising geopolitical tensions, they might have
asked it to hedge the wrong shock. What was more important
in this instance was considering the potential macro
implications of the conﬂict.
Why might gold have underperformed this time? Prior to the
onset of the Middle East conﬂict, gold had rallied to record
levels, passing $5,400 per ounce in January and increasing
over 100% since the start of 2025. We note that it has rallied
before previous shocks as well: It gained 52% between 2006
and the onset of the Great Financial Crisis, and 48% between
2019 and 2021 before the 2022 pullback. However, the extent
to which gold has rallied this time far exceeds those prior
runs, potentially offering insight into why the sell-off was so
great.
Gold's rally was not completely unjustiﬁed. Through 2025, it
beneﬁted from continued central bank demand, as well as
traditional safe-haven demand amid growing geopolitical
tensions, and easing Federal Reserve policy, which encouraged
near-record exchange-traded fund (ETF) buying. Growing
focus on ﬁat currency debasement and the decline in the
dollar's value also drove interest in gold. As the rally
continued into 2026, however, it was getting more difﬁcult to
explain the continued strength. The World Gold Council
recorded an 83% year-over-year increase in investment
demand in 2025, and the initial price performance this year
suggests this speculative momentum had continued, making
the price more vulnerable to shocks.
This was a supply shock rather than a demand shock. Most of

Supply shocks also bring greater inﬂation risks. The Middle

Gold is now much more sensitive to the interest rate and
yield outlook. In recent years, the relationship between gold
and real rates effectively broke down. A shift in central bank
behavior in 2022, combined with growing geopolitical
tensions, reserve diversiﬁcation and strong investment
demand, has supported a continued rally, despite elevated
real yields. Since the start of the conﬂict, however, the
inverse relationship has reappeared. The shock has brought
more focus on inﬂationary risks and the Fed's potential
response, pushing investors back toward the historical
framework that previously governed gold.
Amid the breakdown of the relationship between gold and
real rates, we have also observed a strong inverse correlation
between ETF holdings and the direction of Fed policy. In late
2024, once a clear line of sight to rate cuts was established,
ETFs began buying and subsequently added 800 tonnes in
2025, supporting prices. Their sensitivity to monetary policy
has continued. The Fed held rates ﬂat in March, while the
ongoing conﬂict has lowered expectations for cuts this year,
driving ETFs to sell 90 tonnes in March. With ETFs now the
swing buyer/seller in the gold market, we expect ETF
holdings to be an important price driver this year, with
direction determined by the duration of the conﬂict and
policy response. ■

HEALTH CARE
Testing Longevity
Erin Wright, Equity Analyst, Morgan Stanley & Co. LLC
According to the Centers for Disease Control and Prevention,
the US spends $3.4 trillion annually on chronic diseases when
considering lost economic productivity. We estimate $1.4
trillion of this spending was tied to preventable disease in
2024, underscoring the growing ﬁnancial burden faced by
consumers amid rising out-of-pocket health care costs.
However, expanded adoption of preventative testing,
screening and monitoring—with much of it borne by
consumers themselves—has the potential to meaningfully
bend the cost curve. We estimate anywhere from $200
billion to $800 billion of gross spending on preventable
disease could be avoided by 2050.
We are intrigued by the consumer-driven momentum in
health care and believe shifting consumer priorities will have
meaningful implications for the broader care delivery
ecosystem. As consumers take greater control of their health
care, enabled by technology such as telehealth and wearable
devices, the opportunity expands for earlier intervention and
more proactive engagement.
CONSUMER-DRIVEN TESTING. Consumer-driven diagnostic
testing is growing at a rapid clip in the US, representing a $4
billion market opportunity, having more than doubled since
2021. Growth has been led by initiatives and partnerships
across key public clinical laboratories as well as public and
private industry constituents. There is also scope for further
expansion through wearable devices, imaging and direct-to-
consumer channels, which would broaden the addressable
market and reverberate across the health care continuum.
Based on our AlphaWise survey of consumers, some 34% of
respondents have completed a voluntary lab test in the past
three years. About 41% said they currently use wearable
health/ﬁtness monitoring devices and about a third reported
that they regularly change their habits based on information
from their device.
While health and ﬁtness monitoring technologies are not
new, we view the increasing integration of diagnostic results
and personal biological data into health tracking as
compelling. This integration is enabling a new layer of
innovative services and technology to facilitate interpretation,
accessibility and affordability. For example, in the past year,
one of the major testing companies partnered with companies
that offer wearables and health care platforms.
NO DOCTOR REQUIRED. Direct-to-consumer (DTC) lab
testing began in the early 2000s, when some states began
allowing “direct access testing”: Patients were permitted to
order lab tests without physician requisitions. Personal
genomics companies gained popularity later in the decade. In
2017, the Food and Drug Administration (FDA) approved
23andMe’s Genome Service Genetic Health Risk tests for 10
diseases or conditions, the ﬁrst time a DTC test was allowed
to provide information about genetic predisposition for
certain medical conditions.
In 2014, the Department of Health and Human Services
allowed labs to release test results directly to patients, not
just to ordering providers, and amended privacy rules to give
patients a right of access to their completed lab results,
furthering consumer access and engagement. The COVID-19
pandemic accelerated DTC lab testing, ﬁrst with at-home
sample collection kits and later fully at-home tests. With
COVID as a jump starter, the DTC market continues to
mature, augmented by greater health awareness, a demand
for convenience, the shift toward e-commerce, the adoption
of telehealth and a growing focus on wellness and longevity.
Common tests include general wellness tests such as annual
blood panels, disease-speciﬁc panels, STD/STI testing, allergy
panels, fertility and hormone tests, vitamin levels and more.
At-home collection kits are available for some tests, but most
are done via in-person lab visits. Prices can range from $50
for simple tests to more than $300 for extensive panels.
WEARABLES AND FITNESS DEVICES. Consumer wearables
and ﬁtness devices emerged from a convergence of
miniaturized sensors, mobile computing and behavioral health
trends. Early roots trace back to the 1960s–1990s with
pedometers and heart-rate chest straps used by athletes, but
the modern consumer market began taking shape in the late
2000s. The widespread adoption of smartphones enabled
wearables to function as companion devices, ofﬂoading
computation and visualization to mobile apps. Fitbit’s 2009
launch was an inﬂection point for mass-market ﬁtness
tracking, popularizing step counts, calorie-burn estimates and
gamiﬁed health goals among US consumers. During the
2010s, wearables evolved from simple activity trackers into
multisensor health platforms. Advances in optical heart-rate
monitoring, accelerometers, sleep tracking and later
electrocardiogram and blood oxygen sensors expanded use
cases beyond ﬁtness into wellness and preventative health.
Earlier this year, the FDA issued new guidance stating that
wearable devices and associated software designed to
support general wellness—such as ﬁtness trackers,
smartwatches, activity monitors and wellness apps—will
generally not require formal FDA approval or clearance as
long as they don’t make medical or clinical claims. Devices
that simply provide health information without claiming
clinical accuracy or medical utility are exempt from stringent
FDA oversight. This establishes fewer regulatory hurdles to
bring new products to market. ■

Is California’s Economy Still Golden?
Mark T. Schmidt, CFA, Strategist, Morgan Stanley & Co. LLC
As the biggest issuer of municipal debt in the US, California is
a key credit for a variety of muni investors. That said, the
volatility of its income tax revenues creates a unique
budgetary challenge. The top 1% of the state’s earners pay
nearly half its personal income tax, and this group’s income, in
turn, is highly sensitive to the stock market. While this is an
ongoing structural issue, it has led to starkly different deﬁcit
forecasts for the coming ﬁscal year. Governor Gavin
Newsom’s budget proposal in January estimated a $2.9
billion gap, while California’s nonpartisan Legislative Analyst
Ofﬁce (LAO) has projected an $18 billion shortfall.  
In our view, deﬁcit projections should be taken with a grain of
salt. California’s economy is dynamic, while the LAO’s
forecasts could prove a touch too pessimistic. Although a
cautious approach to forecasting is often prudent amid
uncertainty, in this case we think it undercounts how a strong
equity market could support income tax revenues.
California’s economy remains vibrant. The Bay Area
technology cluster is a remarkable growth engine. The state
also has a number of highly valuable, late-stage, pre-IPO
companies that could sustain tax receipts for at least another
year. And an uptick in IPO volumes could lift California
income taxes for several years.
Away from tech, other indicators point to California’s
economy performing relatively well. In particular, Southern
California shows signs of stabilization after dealing with a
downturn in logistics—notably the operations serving its
ports—and media.
State economies are hard to observe in real time, and we do
not endeavor to forecast state GDP. But by most metrics,
California’s economy still strikes us as robust, and that gives
us conﬁdence in state tax revenues, and therefore credit
quality, for the next 12 months.
There are risks to our view. A stock market correction could
lead to a drop in tax receipts, especially if accompanied by a
big slowdown in IPO activity. But we still see most of the
ﬁscal risks to California’s budget coming from spending and
not from revenue.

With Private Credit Under Scrutiny, Selectivity Is Key
Direct lending continues to make the headlines. Many
investors have expressed concerns about the condition of
underlying loans in business development companies, or
BDCs, and redemption requests have risen sharply. But the
bigger picture around credit and portfolio structure is far
more nuanced. In fact, new opportunities may be emerging
amid the pullback. To offer their long-term perspective, as
well as their take on recent developments, Apollo partners
Earl Hunt and Daniel Vogel discussed the asset class with
Morgan Stanley Wealth Management’s head of Family Ofﬁce
Investments, Daniel Maccarrone. Below is an edited version of
their conversation on April 21.
Daniel Maccarrone (DM): Let’s start with what’s happening in
private credit today. What do you think people are missing in
the debate? 
Earl Hunt (EH): First, I think private credit is being deﬁned
very narrowly. We view it as a vast, dynamic $40 trillion asset
class—spanning corporate and asset-backed credit, the
majority of which is investment grade. In our view, it really
includes any type of asset sitting on a bank’s balance sheet.
We think the strong fundamentals and performance for much
of private credit, which we’ve seen in our own credit books,
have been missed. When you look at the underlying
borrowers, we’ve seen year-over-year growth in revenues and
margin expansion, along with improvement in traditional
coverage statistics—including interest coverage.
Regarding what’s happening today, negative sentiment has
been driven in part by one slice of private credit—namely
levered software lending. Software represents about 25% to
30% of the below-investment grade direct lending universe,
and as people think about the potential ramiﬁcations of AI
adoption, they’re especially concerned about the software
industry. Public software stocks have declined meaningfully,
and now people are trying to ﬁgure out what that means for
valuations, discount rates and yields in privately held
software companies. 
In itself, that’s certainly nothing new. Repricings within
industries, driven by technological innovation or other
changes, happen all the time.
Daniel Vogel (DV): I’d add that we’ve always had a rigorous
approach to private credit at Apollo, from the post-ﬁnancial
crisis period though the COVID crisis and today. As we’ve held
to our principles and stuck to our knitting, we have avoided
more speculative assets for the most part. Not many software
names have come through our investment committees and
when they have, they have been very heavily scrutinized. We
were “risk off” in software well before it was the news of the
day—we had the view early on that AI would be
transformative for the industry.
DM: As an asset manager, how do you view the increase in
redemptions in BDCs? 
EH: The semi-liquid nature of these vehicles is purposefully
designed to match the natural liquidity of the underlying
assets. As per the stated terms of these funds, managers
typically choose to honor redemptions of up to 5% of net
asset value per quarter. This approach ensures that new,
redeeming and remaining shareholders are treated equally.
These environments can create some of the most attractive
opportunities in the credit cycle, but only for managers with
the ﬂexibility to deploy countercyclically.
Already, spreads in the direct lending space have widened
roughly 25 basis points to 50 basis points. We believe this has
created a deployment opportunity for managers. And we see
institutional investors leaning into this opportunity.
DV: We are also starting to see better protections for lenders,
including loan documentation. And that’s much more
important to us than just the spread or rate. Better
protections lead to better outcomes, all else equal.
I don’t think lending standards were too far off from where
they should have been, but they could have been in a year or
two. The standards in private credit were starting to migrate
lower toward parity with public credit, and now we’re moving
back to stronger documentation. I think we’re going to see
better outcomes in private credit than liquid credit because of
that.
DM: Have you seen any surprises in how private credit
structures have behaved—speciﬁcally, evergreen BDCs—as
they work through the redemptions?
EH: We haven’t seen any surprises. We have always felt that
the 5% limit on redemptions was appropriate—especially in
environments like this. We want to be in the position of a
“value investor” and respond to changing market dynamics. If
you choose to honor redemptions above 5%, the manager is
less able to play offense. That said, we do think there needs
to be some re-education for investors on the product and its
liquidity proﬁle, and that is happening.

differently now than in the past—whether due to
competition, the economy or the potential disruption from
AI? 
DV: We’ve employed a common lens for years. We strive to
have disciplined underwriting across all business lines, from
investment grade credit all the way through private equity.
Today, AI impacts are more pronounced than ever, but we’ve
been focusing on risks across portfolios for years. By being

vehicle effectively aligns the ﬁve-year duration of investor

Investors are recognizing the features embedded within the

Investors are going to understand dispersion and that there
are different risk proﬁles within this asset class. Ultimately,
we believe this will lead to further adoption of private credit
in the wealth segment. ■

Beta is a measure of the volatility, or systematic risk, of a security or a portfolio in comparison to the market as a whole.
Correlation This is a statistical measure of how two securities move in relation to each other. This measure is often converted into what isknown as correlation coefﬁcient, which ranges between -1 and +1. Perfect positive correlation (a correlation coefﬁcient of +1) implies that as onesecurity moves, either up or down, the other security will move in lockstep, in the same direction. Alternatively, perfect negative correlationmeans that if one security moves in either direction the security that is perfectly negatively correlated will move in the opposite direction. If thecorrelation is 0, the movements of the securities are said to have no correlation; they are completely random. A correlation greater than 0.8 isgenerally described as strong, whereas a correlation less than 0.5 is generally described as weak.
